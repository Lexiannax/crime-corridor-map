Police liability & misconduct costs
Shiflett v. City of San Leandro · $3.9 million · 2024
On October 6, 2019, Sorrell Shiflett — a man with major cognitive disabilities from a 2008 brain injury — was walking in his neighborhood before sunrise dressed as the anime character Naruto. Officer Ismael Navarro responded to a suspicious persons call and began questioning Shiflett. When Shiflett ran because he was frightened and wanted to get his father to help explain his disability, both officers gave chase. Shiflett stopped, turned around, and walked back toward the officers to surrender. He was beaten with police batons and Tased multiple times despite neither officer claiming he tried to strike them.

The beating left Shiflett with two brain hemorrhages and a separated shoulder requiring multiple surgeries. Officer Navarro failed to save his bodycam footage. Officer Pantoja did not activate his camera until after the use of force occurred.

No criminal charges were ever filed against Shiflett. No criminal charges were ever filed against either officer. The City of San Leandro paid $3.9 million to settle the federal civil rights lawsuit in 2024. The officers remained employed.

The taxpayer cost of this one incident: $3.9 million settlement + years of investigation and legal defense costs + two officers' salaries during and after the incident.
Robustelli v. City of San Leandro · Exposure undisclosed · Active 2025
Former City Manager Fran Robustelli filed a lawsuit in June 2025 in Alameda County Superior Court alleging Councilmembers Azevedo, Simon, and Aguilar pressured her to violate city protocols, misuse taxpayer funds, and surrender administrative authority. The lawsuit also alleges gender discrimination — that the named councilmembers exhibited repeated behavior suggesting "a woman cannot be a city manager." Settlement exposure is undisclosed but lawsuits of this type in California typically settle in the $500,000–$2 million range. This comes directly from city reserves — taxpayer money.
Azevedo federal investigation · Cost to taxpayers · Estimated $500K–$1M
Federal corruption investigations require city legal representation, staff time to respond to subpoenas and document requests, and disruption to city operations. The Azevedo case was connected to the broader Sheng Thao/Duong investigation. Cities in comparable federal investigations typically incur $500,000 to $1 million in legal costs beyond the settlement itself. Azevedo pled guilty to two federal felonies: conspiracy to commit honest services wire fraud and making false statements to the FBI and IRS. He is now a cooperating federal witness. No costs have been publicly disclosed.
Simon improper reimbursements · Amount ordered repaid
District 4 Councilmember Fred Simon submitted unauthorized mileage reimbursement claims for routine travel between his home and City Hall. After internal review he was ordered to repay the funds. He also allegedly sought reimbursement for out-of-state and non-city-related travel including a trip to a homeless shelter in Chicago and a desert water agency in Palm Springs. Additionally, Robustelli's lawsuit alleges Simon improperly collected medical benefits from three public agencies simultaneously — the city of San Leandro, Oro Loma Sanitary District, and a third entity. Exact dollar amounts are subject to the ongoing lawsuit.
How to get the real numbers
California Public Records Act requests
You are entitled to all of this · File at sanleandro.org/184/Public-Records-Requests
Under the California Public Records Act (Cal. Gov. Code §6250) and SB 1421 (2019), you have the legal right to request:

Police misconduct records: All complaints sustained against SLPD officers, all use-of-force incident reports, all officer-involved shooting records, all disciplinary records for sustained misconduct findings.

Settlement records: All payments from the city's risk management fund for liability claims, including police misconduct settlements and personal injury claims.

Payroll data: Full SLPD payroll by officer including base salary, overtime, and benefits. Also available through the California State Controller's Government Compensation database at publicpay.ca.gov.

Workers' compensation: Annual workers' comp expenditures for city employees including police officers.

The city must respond within 10 business days. If they do not, file a complaint with the California Attorney General's office.
